Curated Excerpts — Thinking, Fast and Slow (Daniel Kahneman)

Purpose: Ground selection of one high-leverage model. This pack emphasizes the System 1 vs System 2 architecture, WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is), substitution, and cognitive ease. Avoids deep dives on anchoring, availability, and loss aversion.

1) Two systems of thought
- System 1: Fast, automatic, effortless, associative, often emotional. It generates quick impressions and feelings.
- System 2: Slow, effortful, deliberate, rule-governed, capable of complex computations. It monitors and (sometimes) corrects System 1.
- “Laziness of System 2”: It conserves effort and often endorses System 1’s answers unless a conflict is detected.

2) WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is)
- The mind builds the most coherent story from the information at hand, ignoring what is missing.
- Confidence is often a feeling of coherence, not a measure of accuracy.
- Illusion of validity: Strong internal consistency of a story is mistaken for truth even when evidence is thin.

3) Substitution
- When faced with a difficult question, System 1 unconsciously answers an easier, related question.
- Example: “How happy are you with your life?” → substituted by “What is my mood right now?”
- This mechanism explains many predictable errors under time pressure or cognitive load.

4) Iconic problem — Bat and Ball (Cognitive Reflection Test)
- “A bat and a ball cost $1.10 in total. The bat costs $1.00 more than the ball. How much does the ball cost?”
- Intuitive answer (System 1): 10 cents. Correct answer (System 2): 5 cents (since 1.05 + 0.05 = 1.10).
- Demonstrates how an appealing, immediate response arises and how deliberate checking prevents error.

5) Stroop Task — Triggering System 2
- Reading the word “RED” printed in blue ink and naming the ink color requires inhibiting the automatic reading response.
- The need for inhibition and attention lights up System 2; performance worsens under cognitive load or distraction.

6) Cognitive Ease vs. Strain
- Repetition, clear fonts, and good mood create “ease,” increasing perceived truth and reducing scrutiny.
- Strain (difficult font, low contrast, accountability) can cue System 2 to engage more carefully.

7) Practical levers
- Create friction to slow down snap judgments when stakes are high (checklists, base-rate prompts, red teaming).
- Use structured decision processes (premortems, outside view) to counter WYSIATI and substitution.

8) Additional illustrative example — “Linda Problem” (Conjunction fallacy)
- A description of “Linda” (bright, outspoken, concerned with discrimination) leads many to rate “Linda is a bank teller and active in the feminist movement” as more probable than “Linda is a bank teller.”
- Represents representativeness heuristic and System 1’s love of coherence over logic; System 2 must enforce probability rules.

These excerpts collectively support a single, battle-ready model: Dual-Process Decision Guardrails (System 1 vs System 2 + WYSIATI/substitution), anchored by the bat-and-ball problem and the Stroop task, with operational levers to trigger deliberate thinking when it matters.
