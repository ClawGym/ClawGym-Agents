Background: Atogepant is an oral calcitonin gene-related peptide receptor antagonist under evaluation for the prevention of episodic migraine.

Methods: We conducted a phase 3, multicenter, randomized controlled trial that was double-blind and placebo-controlled. Adults aged 18–70 years with episodic migraine (4–14 monthly migraine days) who met inclusion criteria and had no exclusion criteria were randomized (1:1:1) to atogepant 10 mg, atogepant 30 mg, or placebo once daily for 12 weeks. The primary endpoint was change from baseline in mean monthly migraine days across weeks 1–12. Secondary endpoints included the proportion of patients achieving a ≥50% reduction in monthly migraine days, use of acute migraine medication, and patient-reported outcomes.

Results: A total of 910 participants were randomized (mean age 39 years; 79% women); 872 (96%) completed the trial. Atogepant 10 mg and 30 mg reduced monthly migraine days from baseline by −3.7 and −4.1 days, respectively, versus −2.5 with placebo; treatment differences were −1.2 days (95% CI −1.7 to −0.7; p<0.001) and −1.6 days (95% CI −2.1 to −1.1; p<0.001). Responder rates (≥50% reduction) were 55% and 61% with atogepant versus 39% with placebo (p<0.001). Acute medication days decreased more with atogepant than placebo. Efficacy was consistent across predefined subgroups. Adverse events were generally mild to moderate; the most common were nausea (8–10%), constipation (7–9%), and fatigue (5–6%). Discontinuation due to adverse events occurred in 3–4% with atogepant versus 2% with placebo; serious adverse events were ≤1% and balanced across groups.

Conclusions: Atogepant showed clinically meaningful efficacy and was generally well tolerated for prevention of episodic migraine over 12 weeks. Longer-term safety and durability of effect require further study.
