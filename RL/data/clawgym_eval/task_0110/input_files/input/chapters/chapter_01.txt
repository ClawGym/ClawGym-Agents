Boston’s harbor wind sawed through the alleys, and Eliza pulled her shawl tight as lanterns guttered along the wharf. The boards creaked with the weight of crates and footsteps, with voices pressed low so sound would not carry. Eliza counted her breaths and the distance between guard posts. She kept one eye on the water and another on the shadows. A bell rang upriver, and Eliza flinched, then steadied, waiting for a sign. A gull tore a scrap from a mooring line and rose into the iron sky. Eliza tasted salt and ink and fear; she measured it against the stubborn warmth of the crowd.

Jonah ducked into the lane behind her, bringing the scent of rope and tar. He said her name—Eliza—once, calm, as if to anchor her. She nodded and pointed toward the custom house. Magistrate Perkins had walked by at dusk, coat flicking like a warning. Eliza recalled his lean face and the way he scanned the docks as if columns on a ledger. The memory skittered across her mind and was gone. Mae had slipped a folded broadside to Eliza at noon, hands inked, eyes bright, whispering that the time had finally come. Eliza pressed her palm against the paper in her pocket and felt the presswork ridges through the cloth. She took one step, then another, letting the rhythm of the planks carry her forward. Around her, the harbor breathed in and out, a restless animal that refused the leash.
