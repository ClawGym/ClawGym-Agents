# Notes on why physical albums matter

I grew up flipping through sleeves at the record shop, learning to read a disc’s history from the wear on its edges. When I tour, the table with vinyl and CDs isn’t just merch—it’s a handshake, a signature, a keepsake. Streams vanish into the ether; records sit on shelves, remembered and revisited.

Quotes to consider:
- "Digital streams feel like tap water; vinyl is a shared bottle of wine."
- "Owning an album anchors memory to an object."
- "Imperfections tell a story; scratches are timestamps."

Arguments:
- The physical format encodes intention: artwork size, sequencing, and liner notes are part of the message.
- Physical ownership resists platform churn; files and streams get relabeled, remastered, or removed without notice.
- Value signals matter: paying for a record sets a different expectation than renting a stream.

Anecdote:
Last month, a fan brought a sun-faded copy of my first EP, signed years ago. We talked about the gig where they bought it—how the room smelled, which song I messed up, the friend they came with. That object carried the memory better than any playlist ever could.
