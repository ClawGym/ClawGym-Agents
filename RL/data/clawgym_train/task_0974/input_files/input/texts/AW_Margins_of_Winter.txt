There are seasons when reading becomes a form of shelter. In winter, the margin fattens with thought; the center is for survival. We underline less because the hand is cold, and we write wider because the daylight is short. Between the printed lines, the white grows a fur of graphite and breath.

Annotations are not interruptions; they are temperatures left in the text. A question mark is a thermometer. A bracket is a scarf. We pretend our notes are temporary scaffolds, but when the thaw comes, they remain as sediment: weather made legible.

I have learned that the margin teaches you to wait. To rest in the side of things while the center takes its time. In the radiators’ hiss and the window’s rime, a sentence leans toward its neighbor and asks to be held. The reader answers in the edges, where hospitality can be afforded without spectacle. The winter reader moves slowly, tracing a faint path along the periphery, each comment a footprint that will not be erased by first sun but preserved by the cold’s steady patience.
