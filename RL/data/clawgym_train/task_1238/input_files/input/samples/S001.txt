At dawn, the arroyo whispered through the mesquite, carrying seed stories and grit. My grandmother’s hands mapped the rain that never arrived, tracing constellations of dry wells across a kitchen table. We planted anyway—amaranth, tepary beans, hope—watering with careful syllables, listening for thunder behind the freeway’s ache. Coyotes learned our names. Dust learned our lungs. When the monsoon finally broke, it broke everything: fences, calendars, the old certainty that the land keeps quiet. Mud climbed the porch in small brown prayers. In the radio’s static, a song about beginnings. We swept, we sang, we gathered the scattered seeds and wrote our harvest in the margins of a phone bill. The sky apologized in purple. The cactus bloomed an answer we didn’t expect.
