Lytton Strachey’s prose is not paint, yet it tutors Bloomsbury’s edges. His miniature portraits, clipped and sly, offer an economy of emphasis that the painters translate into line and block. One sees in Grant’s contour or Bell’s flattened plane the wit of Strachey’s sentence: a refusal of pomposity, a pleasure in structural brevity. The satire clears a space where affection can be articulate, not sentimental. It is a discipline of tone that dampens grandeur to a workable amplitude, letting intimacy do the heavy lifting.
