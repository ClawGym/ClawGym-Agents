Title: Early Remote Shift Productivity Impacts by Sector
Author: Norwood & Ames Consulting
Date: 2020-10-15
Type: Consultancy report (industry analysis)

Executive Summary
- Across 180 medium-to-large firms studied during March–August 2020, non‑tech sectors experienced short‑term productivity declines during the abrupt shift to remote work.
- Average changes in output per FTE (seasonally adjusted) over the first 4–6 months of remote operations:
  - Manufacturing back-office and operations coordination: −8% (range −4% to −12%)
  - Call centers (non-automated): −5% to −12% depending on IVR maturity
  - Retail field management: −4%
  - Professional services (excluding software): −2% to +1% (median −1%)
  - Software & IT: +1% to +3% once VPN and CI/CD stability improved
- Primary drivers of declines: device logistics delays, immature VPN capacity, unfamiliarity with remote workflows, increased meeting load (“panic standups”), and caregiving disruptions.
- Conclusions are SHORT-TERM only; we expect adaptation effects within 6–12 months.

Scope and Sample
- Sectors: manufacturing (back-office), retail operations, financial services operations, customer support, professional services, software/IT.
- Regions: North America (72%), Europe (20%), APAC (8%).
- Sample: 180 firms; 142,000 employees; baseline period: Jan–Feb 2020.

Methodology
- Compared pre/post metrics at the team level using:
  - Output per FTE (tickets resolved, units processed, deals closed, commits merged)
  - Quality indicators (rework rate, SLA adherence)
  - Meeting load (hours/week)
- Data sources: timesheet systems, workflow tools (Jira, ServiceNow), ERP throughput logs, call center dashboards.
- Adjustments: seasonality, sick leave spikes, onboarding freeze effects.
- Limitations:
  - Confounding factors (school closures, macro demand shock) not fully isolatable.
  - Rapidly changing IT capacity (VPN, home equipment) during measurement window.
  - Focused on early acclimation; does not reflect matured remote practices.

Findings by Sector
1) Manufacturing Back-Office/Operations
- Output/FTE: −8% average (n=46 firms).
- Notes: Fragmented document workflows and reliance on physical signatures slowed processes. Ad hoc scanning workarounds partially mitigated by July.

2) Call Centers (Non-automated)
- Output/FTE: −5% to −12% (n=28).
- Notes: Home network instability increased average handle time; QA sampling dipped. Firms with softphone training and stipend policies recovered faster.

3) Retail Field Management
- Output/FTE: −4% (n=19).
- Notes: Coordination overhead rose due to shifting local regulations; managers reported 30–50% increase in meetings.

4) Professional Services (Non-IT)
- Output/FTE: −1% median (n=34).
- Notes: Client onboarding delays and document routing hurdles. By August, teams with standardized e‑signature flows returned to baseline.

5) Software & IT
- Output/Developer: +1% to +3% (n=53).
- Notes: After initial VPN congestion, teams with established CI/CD and code review norms saw slight gains; meeting load stabilized by late June.

Timeframe Observations
- First 6–8 weeks: pronounced coordination costs, especially where work depended on legacy on‑prem workflows.
- Weeks 9–24: partial recovery as tooling and norms improved; still below baseline in non‑tech operations.

Recommendations
- Invest in e‑signature and digital intake.
- Reduce meeting load via agenda discipline and async updates.
- Provide home-office stipends and network support.
- Phase rollout with role-specific playbooks.

Interpretation
- The 2020 remote shock produced short‑term declines in non‑tech functions. Knowledge‑intensive, tool‑mature teams fared better even in early months. Long‑term effects require follow‑up once adaptation stabilizes.
