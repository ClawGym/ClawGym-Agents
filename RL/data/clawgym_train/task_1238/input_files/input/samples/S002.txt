Tonight I carry a suitcase of vowels across the strait—
a chapel of breath tucked under the tongue.
Each streetlight pronounces my name differently,
Milo, Milagro, mistake, milagro again.
Between ferries, I count islands like syllables,
paper boats of Tagalog and English dissolving into foam.
My mother taught me to knead silence until it rises.
My father taught me to refuse borders shaped like ropes.
I write a letter on my wrist where sweat can read it:
I belong, I belong, I belong.
Waves keep editing. I keep writing.
