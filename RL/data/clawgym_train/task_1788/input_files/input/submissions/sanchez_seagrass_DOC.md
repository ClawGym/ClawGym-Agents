# DOC Pulses from Seagrass Meadows Adjacent to Coral Reefs

Lead author: M. Sanchez

Motivation: Coral reefs adjacent to seagrass meadows experience strong diel biogeochemical variability. We propose that low-oxygen night-time DOC release from seagrass canopies drives microbial oxygen demand in adjacent reef sands.

Approach: We deployed optical sensors at the meadow-reef interface and collected surface water at dawn and dusk over two weeks. Dissolved organic carbon increased rapidly near the meadow edge following sunset. DOC concentrations rose by approximately 20–30 µM alongside declines in dissolved oxygen. We also measured flow to estimate residence times and mixing.

Preliminary interpretation: The timing of DOC pulses suggests exudation and/or resuspension of labile carbohydrates that fuel respiration in permeable sediments downstream. While these signals are consistent, we did not yet quantify variability across events or evaluate uncertainty formally. Future sampling will address spatial gradients and microbial uptake kinetics with incubations.
