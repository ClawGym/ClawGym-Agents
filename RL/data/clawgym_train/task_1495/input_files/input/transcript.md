Title: The Lantern Man of Graybridge
Episode: S4E03
Recording date: June 12, 2026
Location: Graybridge, Maine — on-site and studio blend

[MUSIC: low, briny drones, gulls, distant foghorn]

MAYA (host): At 3:17 a.m., the light in Breakwater Lighthouse winked out for sixteen seconds. The Coast Guard log recorded a brief outage, a corrective restart, and then a note that still turns stomachs in this town: “Keeper unresponsive—investigation initiated.” Inside the keeper’s room, a coffee mug still steamed and a hand-scrawled message near the door read, “Went to help.” Help who? That is where our story begins.

DEV (co-host): I am standing on cracked granite at the base of the old breakwater. The lighthouse is maybe a quarter mile ahead, a white needle against a sheet of pewter Atlantic. The locals call this sliver of coast the narrow throat. Currents collide here. Stories collide here, too.

MAYA: Welcome to Echoes & Evidence. I am Maya Tan, and with me is my co-host, Dev Patel. Today, we are in Graybridge, Maine—population eight thousand and change—chasing a twenty-eight-year-old disappearance that fused Coast Guard memos with campfire legend. Our guide through the case is a former detective who still keeps a corkboard in her linen closet.

[LIGHT LAUGH]

MAYA: Detective Lena Ortiz, welcome back to the show.

LENA (guest, former detective): Thanks, Maya. Graybridge is a place that wears its weather like a mood. If you hear the foghorn behind me, that is the tone of this case.

---

DEV: The official record centers on October 14th, 1998. Breakwater Light had two personnel on rotation: head keeper Jonah Parr, forty-six, and assistant keeper Eloise Hale, twenty-three. Hale was a maritime student from inland Vermont, here on a semester placement that turned into a full-season contract. Parr grew up in Graybridge. He liked chess and strong tea. He also liked rules.

MAYA: That night brought a moonless fog, the kind locals say eats sound. At 2:58 a.m., Parr radioed the station to note visibility at less than a quarter mile and request a check on a buoy light to the south. At 3:17, the beam dipped. Sixteen seconds. Then up again.

LENA: The first responders found the entry door propped with a paint can. A boot scuff suggested someone pushed through quickly, maybe with hands full. On a nail just inside the jamb was a chalkboard used for to-do notes—wicks, oil levels, weather. On it, a smudged message: “Went to help.” That is it. No name. No direction. Just that.

DEV: On his desk was a letter addressed to his sister, Lidia Parr, that he never got to mail. He wrote about the wind that “bit holes through the fog.” On the last line, he wrote, “If the lantern shows you two lights offshore, do not answer the second.”

MAYA: And that is where legend creeps in. The Lantern Man. Graybridge has whispered that name for more than a hundred years.

---

LENA: The story goes that in 1913, a fisherman rigged a lantern to mimic a skiff in distress. Unsuspecting keepers and captains would row out to help and get robbed of cargo. People say one keeper never came back. But the archive only hints at theft investigations—nothing about vanishings. It is slippery. Still, in a town like this, the story sticks.

DEV: Ferry Captain Tom Rourke—then a deckhand—filed a statement in ’98 that he saw a dim light moving parallel to the breakwater around three a.m., too slow for a boat in those currents, too steady for a bobbing lantern on a line. He described it as “a second eye” near the waterline. The Coast Guard annotated his statement with a question mark.

MAYA: Slippery. But here is something you can pin to a board. At 3:21, a neighbor in the cottage row south of the breakwater reported hearing “a shout that stopped short” and the clatter of metal—maybe a boathook. By 3:30, patrol arrived. Parr and Hale were gone. Rowboat missing. Life rings still in place.

LENA: Prints? Yes, but useless. The stone holds patterns from hundreds of boots. Oil stain? Maybe from a lantern wick or a maintenance can. Heavy fog wiped edges.

---

MAYA: We walked to the library where head librarian Erin Beale has curated a small archive on local maritime history. On a top shelf, a thick folder labeled “Breakwater Incident” shared space with a shoebox of index cards. The library smell in Graybridge is a blend of salt and old glue.

ERIN (librarian): When I took over, people kept asking for “the Lantern Man file,” so I made one. Folks love stories more than they love facts. My job is to keep both on separate shelves.

DEV: Those shelves yielded something new. In April of this year, someone dropped a box of old high school notebooks at the front desk. Erin opened one and found an entry labeled “October ’98—pier prank.” It describes two seniors rigging a floodlight with a car battery under the east pier and angling it into the fog to “freak out the keepers.” They brag that the beam hovered and slid like a ghost eye.

MAYA: That sent Lena to track down both men, now in their forties. One answered the door. The other sent a terse email. Both confirmed they set up a light near the town pier for less than fifteen minutes, around three a.m., then fled when they heard a car they thought might be a patrol.

LENA: They say they never went near the breakwater itself. And they swear they did not see Jonah or Eloise. That confession explains a “second light”—maybe the one Tom Rourke saw. It does not explain two missing people.

---

DEV: Let us talk about Eloise. Her roommate at the boarding house, Camilla Ng, remembers Eloise as steady and stubborn. Eloise wrote in her internship journal about protocols: “Do not leave your post without notifying station.” But she also wrote about wanting “to prove I can respond under pressure.”

MAYA: Her mother still keeps Eloise’s clippings in a neat envelope. We spoke off-mic. She remembers a phone call a week before the disappearance when Eloise laughed about Jonah’s tea kettle whistling like “a mad piccolo.” It is the mundane details that break you in half.

LENA: Jonah’s sister Lidia, in her grief, kept pushing investigators to search deeper offshore and to consider smuggling. In the late ’90s, gray-market cigarettes moved through small harbors like this one. If Jonah saw a signal, he might have gone to confront it. Or to help. He wrote “help,” not “stop.”

DEV: Remember his weird warning about two lights? In his letter, he says “do not answer the second.” If you were raised here, you were raised on Lantern Man parables. Jonah was not naïve. So why did he go?

MAYA: Maybe because he was not reading the legend. Maybe he was reading the weather. A second light could mean someone misfired a flare. It could mean a reflection.

---

LENA: Timelines matter. From 2:58 to 3:30, we have radio logs, a brief outage, a neighbor report, and a patrol arrival. Weather buoy data shows a wind shift at 3:12 from east-northeast to due east. The breakwater’s lee would have turned into a vacuum that sucked fog into a darker hole. In that pocket, sound travels strangely. A shout could sound close when it is not.

DEV: We requested case documents via FOIA. Among heavily redacted pages was an addendum noting “unverified report of a trawler without running lights at 03:05, three cables south.” The margin reads, “likely spurious—no radar confirmation.”

MAYA: We tracked down the watch officer who wrote that. He is retired, lives inland now, likes gardening and sudoku. He told us that back then, losing a keeper was a scandal no one wanted. He also said, “I could not shake the feeling we missed a boat.”

LENA: Here are the leading theories we ran into:

1) Smuggling gone wrong. Jonah went to “help,” found gun runners or bootleggers, and both he and Eloise were forced aboard. But you would expect some trace—drag marks, a struggle, something more than a chalk scribble.

2) Heroic error. A prank light or a reflection mimicked a distress call. Protocols bent. They rowed out fast, hit submerged rock, capsized silently in thick fog. Hypothermia would take them quickly. But the rowboat was never found, and searches dragged the channel relentlessly.

3) Flight. People float theories of staged disappearance: insurance, debts. But bank records and family interviews do not mirror that. Jonah had a garden plot reserved for spring. Eloise had a train ticket home for Thanksgiving.

4) Paranoia, legend-driven misjudgment. The stories got into their heads. But legends do not make rowboats vanish. Currents do.

DEV: The teen diary complicates things. If a staged light existed, it maps onto the second-light warning, onto the deckhand’s statement, and onto a culture primed to chase ghost lanterns. But it also gives the town a scapegoat—a couple of kids—to glue to a complicated hurt.

---

MAYA: We wanted to feel the distance. At dusk, we walked the breakwater with permission from the harbor master. Fog rolled in, not thick, but honest enough. The light is automated now. A metal door where a wooden one used to be. Inside, the stairs spiral like a loose seashell. Graffiti under a landing: J.P. + E.H. Most likely not them. The paint is too new.

LENA: Standing there, I kept thinking about that chalk note. Chalk is the choice of someone who does not want to waste time. Chalk is what your hand grabs when you cannot find a pen. The word “help” looks different when you write it with chalk—powdery, urgent.

DEV: If they left to help, who were they helping? The answer might be simple and sad: no one. They were helping a trick of light and cold water. Or maybe they were answering an old story that wore a new battery and fresh wire.

MAYA: We called Tom Rourke. He is seventy now, gentler than his statement reads. He said, “People here needed the Lantern Man. Better that than admitting the ocean keeps what it wants.”

LENA: Erin the librarian had a different take. She pointed to a shelf with storm accounts and told me, “We tell stories to make patterns out of harm. The files live beside the fables for a reason.”

---

DEV: We close with one small, stubborn detail. The clock in the keeper’s room was wind-up, not electric. When patrol arrived at 3:30, the second hand stuck for a beat at the twelve, then kept ticking. Someone had wound it recently. It is silly, I know. But it means someone took care of the ordinary things that night.

MAYA: Maybe that is the hardest part of all this: how ordinary the last hour must have felt. Tea, wind, fog. A light dips, then rises. A door swings open. Chalk scrapes wood. The rest is silence and salt.

LENA: If you are listening from Graybridge—and we know many of you are—send us your memories of Jonah and Eloise. Not rumors. Memories. Let the record hold more than the gap.

MAYA: Detective Ortiz, thank you for walking out here with us and for sitting at those kitchen tables and for keeping your closet corkboard ready.

LENA: Always.

DEV: We are going to post copies of the public documents we obtained on our website. The teen diary excerpt will be annotated to protect privacy. And if you were one of those kids, you know who you are. You have nothing to gain and nothing to fear by telling the whole story now.
