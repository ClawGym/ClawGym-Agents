Audience
- Primary: Mid-market Operations and RevOps leaders at B2B SaaS companies (50–500 employees)
- Secondary: Finance and IT stakeholders who must approve automation (CFOs, IT managers, compliance owners)
- Technical profile: Tool-savvy but not full-time developers; can evaluate risk and read logs, prefer low/zero-code
- Buying triggers: Reduce manual busywork, fewer errors, faster handoffs, auditability, predictable changes
- Biggest anxieties: Silent failures, brittle integrations, migration risk, lack of approval gates, vendor hype

Decision Criteria & Objections
- Must integrate with Salesforce, Slack, and common SaaS tools without engineering bottlenecks
- Needs audit trail, role-based approvals, and rollbacks for finance/compliance comfort
- Values reliability over novelty; prefers conservative change management
- Objection patterns: “Edge cases will break” “We can do this with scripts/Zapier” “We can’t risk downtime”

Brand Voice & Tone
- Voice: Calm, competent, and specific; confident without bravado
- Tone: Plain English, concrete outcomes, measured optimism; never breathless or hypey
- Diction: Use precise numbers, timeframes, and named systems; avoid vague superlatives
- Length: Tight and economical; no filler, no exclamation marks
- POV: User-centered; focus on their outcomes (“you”) more than “we”
- Consistency: Maintain the customer’s authentic voice in quotes; clarify, tighten, and ground in specifics

Messaging Pillars
1) Reliability you can audit — logs, approvals, rollbacks provide control and visibility
2) Connects to your stack — first-class Salesforce, Slack, and common SaaS integrations
3) Edge-case ready — conditional logic and guardrails for real-world exceptions
4) Time back to the team — measurable reductions in manual work and error rates
5) Safe change — no-surprises deployment and easy reversibility

Style Guide (Do / Don’t)
- Do: Use concrete metrics (e.g., “6 hours to 45 minutes,” “28% backlog drop”)
- Do: Name systems and steps (Salesforce, Slack, approval gates, audit trail)
- Do: Emphasize safety and control (alerts, approvals, rollback)
- Do: Keep sentences short and scannable; prefer verbs over adjectives
- Don’t: Overpromise or use hype (“revolutionary,” “game-changing,” “insane”)
- Don’t: Use exclamation points or informal slang
- Don’t: Introduce jargon that a non-engineer can’t parse
- Don’t: Invent results; keep numbers plausible and traceable

Psychology Alignment (for later application)
- Social Proof: Showcase peers (Ops/RevOps/Finance) and concrete outcomes to reduce risk perception
- Loss Aversion: Frame the cost of inaction (hours lost, errors, silent failures) for conservative buyers
- Authority: Highlight audit trails, approvals, and compliance safeguards to satisfy Finance/IT
- Paradox of Choice: Keep options simple; “good-better-best” or a single recommended path
- Goal-Gradient: Show quick wins (day-one setup, week-one results) to build momentum

Compliance Notes
- Keep testimonials authentic; do not fabricate names or inflate claims beyond what’s stated
- If adding metrics for clarity, use the customer’s own numbers or transparent aggregates
- Avoid naming regulated frameworks unless explicitly approved (e.g., HIPAA, SOC 2)

CTA Language (house style)
- “Start a free trial” / “See it with your data” / “Add an approval gate” / “Enable alerts and rollback”

Use these notes to preserve voice and credibility when refining testimonials.
