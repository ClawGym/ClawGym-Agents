# Drinking to Bind the City: Wine and Society in Antiquity

This paper explores how communal drinking practices shaped social bonds and civic identities in the ancient Mediterranean. Within both Greek and Roman worlds, the cup was more than a vessel: it was a device to choreograph speech, rank, and ritual.

![Attic krater depicting a symposium scene](images/krater.jpg)

In classical Greece, drinkers typically consumed wine undiluted in order to enhance conviviality and display fortitude, and the symposiarch enforced the tempo of cups accordingly. The party was a place where youth became citizens by learning to speak, sing, and joke in properly measured ways.

This paper will kind of show that parties were a big deal, obviously. The Romans did things differently, like eating on couches and drinking like pros; their convivium projected power and hierarchy in a very direct way.

Aside from conviviality, ritualized cups also marked sacred time and space, whether honoring the gods or binding military companions. Banquet vessels and settings signal economic and political networks that knit together city, countryside, and empire.[1]

Yet one should not assume uniformity: practice varied by era, region, and occasion, and hospitality could be both inclusive and exclusionary. Formal performance games at the symposium (including song contests and drinking games) intersected with law, education, and gender norms in ways still debated.[2]
