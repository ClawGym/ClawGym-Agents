# Evaluating Biological Carbon Pump Efficiency in the NE Pacific

Lead author: H. Lee

Background: The biological carbon pump exports particulate organic carbon (POC) from the euphotic zone to depth, but bloom dynamics and stratification modulate export efficiency regionally. Here we integrate sediment trap fluxes with 234Th-238U disequilibrium to quantify 100 m POC export during spring bloom conditions offshore the NE Pacific.

Hypothesis: We hypothesize that stratification-intensified spring blooms increase 100 m POC export by at least 40% relative to winter baseline conditions.

Methods: We deployed cylindrical sediment traps at 100 and 150 m and measured 234Th deficits from 0–150 m to derive independent export estimates. We conducted 14C primary production incubations and measured POC with elemental analysis. We sampled 12 paired profiles (n=12) over two cruises, with 3 trap replicates per depth. Mesoscale context was characterized by satellite chlorophyll and in situ stratification indices.

Results: Export at 100 m averaged 62 ± 9 mg C m^-2 d^-1 during blooms (95% CI), versus 41 ± 7 mg C m^-2 d^-1 in winter. Thorium-derived fluxes agreed within uncertainty (mean bias 6%). Export efficiency (export/production) increased from 0.10 to 0.17. Uncertainty was propagated via Monte Carlo draws of trap catch and 234Th, and confidence intervals (CI) are reported with assumptions stated.

Implications: Bloom-driven increases in POC export likely reflect aggregation and ballasting. Uncertainty remains in shallow remineralization rates, but variance decomposition suggests trap collection error contributes <20% of total variance.
