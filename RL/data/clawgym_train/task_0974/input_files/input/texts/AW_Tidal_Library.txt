At low tide, the library windows blurred with a fine sugar of salt. Each morning, I unlocked the east door to a shiver of paper, the card catalog breathing as if it had lungs. The tide remembers what the shelves forget. I dusted the atlas cases while gulls called down the shoreline, their throats full of small demands, their shadows breaking over the reading tables like brief clouds.

I reorganized the spines by the drag of the moon, a catalog of pull and release. A stranger asked if there was a logic to it, and I said there was, but that it changed with the season. On spring mornings the dictionaries drifted toward the windows; in autumn, the diaries grew heavy and refused to be moved. Salt threaded the pages with a brightness that didn't belong to the index; it belonged to the morning.

The patrons came with maps folded into the kinds of squares the ocean doesn't recognize. They asked for instructions on how to return to themselves, and I sent them to the biography shelves with a pencil. I was not archiving books; I was archiving the way the body returns to itself when it is named. If a wave could be bound in cloth, I would have shelved it between tide tables and almanacs and trusted memory to keep the place.
