The Tideglass of Heron Quay
by L. J. Mallory

Prologue

The tideglass arrived in a shoebox lined with old concert tickets and the corner of a map that had been torn and retaped so many times it had more folds than paper. It clinked once against the cardboard as Mara lifted it out—no bigger than a teacup, faintly storm-cloud gray, its surface veined like the inside of a shell. It was heavier than she expected, and warm despite the draft crawling under the weather-stripped door of the Records Office. When she held it near the window that faced the harbor, the glass trembled in her hands and a ripple of light passed through it, revealing nothing at all at first except the ghost of her own worried face.

Heron Quay had been counted and recounted through storms and holidays and the slow thinning of the herring. Every morning, someone wrote the sea down: heights, winds, temperatures, the names of the boats that creaked out and those that limped back. Mara had been that someone for seven years, since the old keeper Hobb’s eyes went milky and his hand began to tremble. She kept the tide table as if it were a prayer. Numbers steadied her. Patterns meant the world could be learned and maybe even kept.

But when she set the tideglass on the sill, the veneer of certainty she had polished around herself began to crack. The glass hummed—a note that made her molars ache and her eyes water—and a faint silvery script crawled across its surface, as if the sea itself were trying to write back.

Chapter One: A Keeper of Small Certainties

Mara’s fingers remembered every pencil she had dulled writing numbers in the thick Ledger of Heron Quay. When the ledger’s spine cracked, she bound it with twine. When the ink froze in January, she breathed warmth into the nib. When the news from down the coast told of piers ripped up like loose teeth, she pinned their tide tables alongside her own until the wall above her desk looked like a quilt of wind and water.

“Is that a crystal ball?” Elias asked the first time he saw the tideglass. He leaned in the doorway smelling like brine and engine grease. He was a diver who mended nets and checked moorings and retrieved the occasional phone that leapt from careless hands into the harbor’s green mouth.

“It’s a piece of litter someone sent me,” Mara said.

“Litter that sings,” he said, tilting his head. “Hobb said your office had taken up music.”

Mara resisted looking at the window, where the glass glinted like a watchful fish eye. “It hums when the pressure drops,” she said, though she wasn’t sure that was true.

Hobb shuffled in later with the careful high steps that came when his knees were bad. “Used to be a bell in the old lighthouse,” he said, unasked. “Could feel a storm before it announced itself, the metal thrumming under your hand like a trapped bird.”

“Metal sings,” Elias said. “Glass sings. The sea never shuts up.”

“The sea speaks,” Hobb corrected, and sat down to wait for the kettle. “Also: the sea lies. Ask anyone who ever read a calm morning and found a gale at noon.”

Mara wrote down the afternoon’s numbers. The sea did not lie, she thought; it only held too much to be caught by one person’s hand.

Chapter Two: The Minister’s Envelope

The envelope that brought the tideglass bore the seal of the Ministry of Coastal Infrastructure, a gull perched on a stylized wave. The letter inside thanked Heron Quay for its regular reports. It asked whether the town might consent to “community partnership” as a demonstration site for a modular surge barrier, because rising seas required “bold synergies,” and their models suggested Heron Quay qualified as a “Tier Two Risk Zone” and “key candidate for resilient pilot.”

Mayor Darya pinned the letter to the corkboard in the corner of the Dockside Café, beside pictures of the last summer the anchovy run had filled the bay. “They’ll pay,” she said, rubbing the bridge of her nose. “They’ll hire. They’ll pour concrete and call us a test, and if the walls hold, maybe our children won’t need to learn to swim with tool belts on.”

The town grumbled with one voice and a dozen others. Some wanted the barrier because the storms had grown stranger, because the memory of the surge that took Isla’s house still stung salt-sharp. Others wanted to leave the ocean’s edge pliant as it always had been, unbarricaded by sleek, city-made panels that arrived with instructions only engineers could read.

Elias caught Mara outside the café as people spilled onto the cobbles arguing about dredging and taxes. “It’s not just a wall,” he said. “It’s a hinge. You fix it in place and the bay opens and closes by someone else’s timetable.”

“I record the tide,” Mara said, as if that excused her from other kinds of decisions.

“You are their almanac,” he said softly. “You are the hinge they trust.”

She wanted to say she only wrote what the moon and wind dictated, but it was too late for that. The tideglass had begun to show her other things.

Chapter Three: The Glass Writes Back

At first, the glass shimmered with the tide curve like a sighing arch, the numbers Mara knew by heart engraved across its skin. When she aligned it square with the harbor mouth, the line sharpened into intervals of five: 0.4 m, 0.8 m, 1.2, cresting at 3.9 before falling away. When she angled it, the curve blurred, as if the sea shrugged.

Then came the second script, not numbers but tiny brushstrokes that slid beneath each other, rewriting themselves as Mara breathed. They were not a language she recognized, but they held the cadence of annotated memory; the strokes clustered near dates she had circled in the ledger—storm days, days of fish enough for everyone, the day Hobb stumbled on the stairs and laughed it off, the day Elias dove too deep and came up dizzy and stubborn.

She discovered that if she set a finger along the glass and whispered a question, the strokes rearranged to sketch a faint picture within the gray. She learned, grudgingly, that the glass offered glimpses: not certainties, not prophecies, but scenes pulled as if from a tide pool where the sky’s reflection sloshes among the rocks. If she asked about the barrier, the glass showed a parade of concrete panels shouldering out of the belly of a truck, and children racing on top of them while adults argued below. If she asked what the harbor would look like in twenty years, the glass splashed a dozen possibilities across itself: a marina with expensive boats and a sculpture of a gull that never moved; a wild tangle of reeds with canoes nosing the edges; nothing at all but horizon and deeper horizon.

Mara told no one. For three nights, she asked the glass small questions: When will the herring return? Will Hobb’s knees improve when the weather warms? Will Elias ever stop bringing her shells as if she were a tourist who needed proof of the sea?

On the fourth night, she asked what the water would do. The glass sang sharp. It showed not a curve but a surge: an out-of-season storm with its shoulders hunched and its mouth full of broken ice. The harbor rose in the glass, and Darya’s parlor filled to the third step, and Isla’s new house, still smelling of sap and sawdust, sat under a slick of gray water like a drowned table.

Mara set the glass down as if it were a coal pulled from the fire. She slept badly, and when the sun reached into her room she reached back for her coat and her notebook and the old mechanical pencil Hobb had given her when she took the job. Numbers steadied her. She kept counting.

Chapter Four: The Company Men

The Ministry’s engineers arrived with clean boots and a fondness for acronyms. They set up a model on a folding table in the community hall, a bellypond of plastic and blue dye and little gray blocks meant to represent the barrier modules. They poured dyed water down a sluice to mimic a storm surge and then raised the little gray blocks and pronounced the demonstration a success.

“We project a ninety-three percent reduction in flood risk for Heritage Quarter under our proposed phasing,” said the lead, a woman with braided hair and a smile that did not show teeth. “In addition to employment during construction, the barrier doubles as a public promenade. Opportunities for placemaking—”

“The bay is not a place to be made,” Hobb said sharply from the third row.

“Mr. Hobbs—”

“Hobb,” he corrected.

“Hobb,” she said. “The barrier would be controlled in collaboration with local authorities. We are not in the business of stealing community agency.”

Mara felt the tideglass hum in her satchel, slung against her hip like a sleeping animal that dreamed of running.

Elias ran a finger along the edge of the model pool, leaving a low mark in its meniscus. “And when the surge comes from somewhere you didn’t measure?” he asked. “From the back marsh, maybe. From the ground, under the promenade you built for tourists to gawk at?”

“We will install sub-surface drains,” the lead said. “And pumps. There are always funds for pumps.”

“Until the funds are cut,” Darya muttered.

The engineers promised another meeting in a week to discuss details. Outside, the bay lay flat as a held breath. The sky, against the forecast, tasted metallic.

Chapter Five: A Storm With No Name

The storm did not wait for the meeting. It came three days later under a pearly noon that smelled like old pennies. Clouds stacked like rough masonry and then melted into a single smear. The first drops fell fat as plums and were followed by a wind that had not been promised by any of the models. The swell from offshore arrived before any boat had time to reconsider its moorings.

Mara watched the barometer’s needle dive and felt the tideglass vibrate. She had been prepared—because she was always prepared—to call the siren, to tell Harbor Patrol to move the tinnies, to remind Hobb to take the long steps up to his apartment not the shorter, steeper ones on the back of the bakery. But she had not prepared what to say when people asked whether to stack sandbags at the fish market or on the downhill side of the clinic.

The glass showed them both drowning.

“Don’t,” she told it, fast and furious, as if she could scold an instrument for telling the kind of truth that paralyzes. “Not both, not everything.”

Elias burst into the office with his hood dripping. “We’re moving the skiffs to higher ground,” he said. “Isla’s house has plenty of hands. The fish market’s understaffed.”

“Clinic?” Mara asked.

“Priya’s got the staff and their generator is strong,” he said. “But if the doors swell and stick—”

The glass sang a note that hurt Mara’s teeth. She grabbed it and held it like a talisman and asked it a small question: Not where the water would go, not the cruelty of a precise picture of loss, but the tiniest possible hinge she could nudge: If three more people go to the market now, what changes?

The gray of the glass lightened, as if moonlight lay inside it. The brushstrokes rearranged. She saw the fish market door, swollen, jammed, then pushed open with shoulders under an old beam. She saw crates stacked on the counter and salt bags piled in a wall. She saw, later, a ripple of water curling around a newly bought freezer and withdrawing with a sigh.

“Market,” she said, and then louder, into the radio: “Market. You, you, and you. Go now.”

Hobb’s voice crackled. “On my way, Keeper,” he said. The honorific under the old word warmed her in a way the kettle could not.

The storm made chaos of their hours. The promenade proposal made no difference, because boards and panels imagined across meetings did not materialize in a week. The old breakwater did what it had always done, then failed where it had always been weak. The surge came, not as a single wave but as a mouthful of the ocean that could not get enough in one bite.

Mara consulted the glass only when she had a specific hinge to test. If they shored up the bakery with tarps now, would the ovens still be dry tomorrow? The glass showed flour damp, but dough rising under blankets. If Hobb went the long way around to the clinic, would he stumble on the slick steps and break a hip? The glass showed him slow and steady on the longer stairs, wind sneaking up his coat but his hands braced on the rail, his knee objecting but not snapping. If Harrison, who had a bad shoulder from an ancient rugby match, climbed onto the ladder to the bell of the old church, would he fall? The glass’s stroke stuttered; it offered three answers, all contradiction, and Mara told Elias to go instead.

The small hinges added up. They kept the market cool enough, the boats far enough, the shelter warm enough. They did not save Isla’s new house; the water lapped the baseboards and then the second step and then the fourth, and when a wave pushed the couch into the wall, the back leg splintered with the sound of a small tree giving up.

When the wind turned and the barometer’s needle crept up, Mara’s hands shook so hard she spilled tea down her front. Elias took the tideglass gently from her lap and set it on the sill. Neither of them looked to see what it would show next. Outside, the water sneered once and then slunk back.

Chapter Six: The Morning After

There is a violence to a storm that numbers cannot account for, and there is a gentleness to the morning after that numbers do not deserve. Sunlight came like balm. The air smelled of silt and bread dough and diesel. Children in borrowed boots poked sticks into puddles full of broken sky. The café opened with a limited menu and a bottomless pot, and Mayor Darya wept for twenty seconds and then ordered everyone to sit before their knees failed them.

The Ministry engineers arrived with hard hats and cameras, as if they might record the damage into compliance. They spoke softly with Darya, and then softly again with the owners of the houses most damaged, and then loudly into their phones. They did not look Mara in the eye, and she did not offer them numbers.

Hobb wheeled himself into the Records Office during the afternoon lull, his cane balanced across his lap. He stared at the tideglass, which had gone still and almost colorless. “Did it help?” he asked.

“It told me little things,” Mara said. “I stopped asking it for pronouncements. I asked about hinges. Whether the freezer would live. Whether Harrison would fall.”

“And did it tell you whether to build a wall?” Hobb asked.

“It didn’t care,” Mara said. “It showed me a wall that kept out one storm and trapped the flood from a summer thunderhead. It showed me no wall and boats in the square and also no wall and reeds by the steps of the café where people tied their canoes.”

Hobb nodded. “The sea lies,” he said again, and then relented. “No. The sea tells too many truths at once.”

Elias leaned in the doorway with two mugs. “They’re eager,” he said, meaning the engineers. “They want a win they can put on a website and a plaque.”

“And what do we want?” Mara asked.

“I want the old pier fixed,” Elias said. “I want funding for scap nets so the little fish have a rest stop on their way in. I want pumps that someone who knows this place can turn on and off without a permission slip. I want to stop calling everything a pilot.”

“I want to keep writing our own tide,” Mara said.

Chapter Seven: The Meeting That Followed the Storm

They gathered again in the hall—the same folding chairs, the same model bellypond now stained with real silt from boots. The engineers presented their revised plan: fewer panels, taller ones; a sluice gate; a promise for solar backup power that everyone in the room knew would arrive two years late or not at all. Their models had not accounted for ground seep, they said, but now they would.

Darya had prepared her own list, but she set it aside. She pointed at Mara. “Keeper?” she asked.

Mara stood, aware of the way her hands wanted to find a pencil even in a room where no ledger waited. The tideglass had been dull since the surge, as if it had given itself a fever fighting the storm. She cleared her throat. “We will not be a shelf for your trial,” she said carefully. “We will accept funding for repairs. We will accept grants for pumps and drains and the fixing of the old pier. We want help to plant the upper marshes so the next surge has to climb a little hill made of roots. We will discuss a barrier when it is designed with us, not dropped down from a truck pre-measured for someone else’s bay.”

A murmur—a wind beginning.

“We are not against walls,” she said. “We are against being locked out of them.”

The engineers frowned, but not unkindly. The lead took a breath as if she had practiced swallowing harder sentences. “We can revisit,” she said. “We can send two of our planners here for a month. We can give you the models to run.”

Elias raised his hand. “We already run models,” he said, and grinned. “We call them winter.”

Laughter broke some of the tension. Mara felt her heart slow from its thin rabbit stampede.

After the meeting, the lead engineer lingered near the door. “That instrument in your office,” she said without preface. “The humming thing.”

Mara considered lying. “An heirloom,” she said instead. “It shows me what we already know, if we are quiet.”

The engineer looked as if she wanted to ask for a loan. Mara looked as if she wanted to forget she had said anything. The engineer nodded. “Be wary of hinges,” she said, surprising Mara. “It is possible to make a thousand good decisions and still find yourself at a door that will not open.”

“It’s possible to oil the hinge,” Mara said.

“Sometimes the door was cut wrong,” the engineer replied. “But yes. Oil helps.”

Chapter Eight: The Quiet Work

Repair work is quieter than storm work and much longer. They swept mud out of houses and sand out of gears. The freezer at the fish market sighed back to life thanks to tarps that had held, a dozen hands, and mercy. The clinic’s doors were planed and rehung with room for swelling in the heart of August. The old pier was bolted and braced. Kids painted signs that said HIGH WATER with arrows to mark the floodline. Someone put one of those signs in Mara’s office, and she left it there, not as a taunt but a record.

At night, she set the tideglass on the sill. The strokes that had once rearranged quickly now moved like grass in slow wind. When she asked small questions, it still answered, but more of its answers looked like weather maps rather than vignettes: broad movements, not precise doors.

She found she preferred it. The storm had shown her the danger of sharp images without context. She told Elias, who nodded and said he had always hated crystal balls in stories; they made people lazy. “A good tide table makes you look,” he said. “So does your glass, if you use it for questions and not absolutions.”

Hobb’s knees ached less as the weather warmed. He still refused to use the shorter, steeper stairs. He told stories in the café of a storm that had arrived in the year most names remembered as The Year Without a Summer. He told how his grandmother had taught him to read tea leaves and then insisted that the leaves were only a way to get you to think about what you already wanted to do.

“You always wanted to be Keeper,” he told Mara one morning when only three customers filled the room and the kettle wheezed. “You just had to tell yourself a story in numbers to give yourself permission.”

Mara considered the ledger, the glass, the stretch of water outside. “I wanted to keep,” she admitted. “Things. Places. Even the way the wind smells on a cold morning. It is foolish. The sea takes.”

“Sometimes the sea returns,” Hobb said. “It is not reliable. But neither are we.”

Chapter Nine: An Atlas for the Small

By summer, the Ministry’s planners had moved into the old rooms above the bakery, and the town learned their names. The barrier became a sketch in the corner of a chalkboard and then, haltingly, a set of measurements that included spaces left for the habits of eels and the way fog rolled into the bay in July. A drain was installed where the back marsh accepted the stormwater from the school’s parking lot. A decision was made not to pave a section of Heron Street after someone noticed how quickly puddles soaked into its old gravel.

Mara began a second ledger, thinner than the first and bound in a cover Elias made with a sailmaker’s needle. She titled it Atlas of Heron Quay: Small Hinges. She wrote in it not only the tide and the winds, but the name of the volunteer who learned how to prime the pump without jamming it, the day the scap nets went in, the number of times Hobb’s cane clacked on the long stairs without incident. She recorded the first herring run to spill silver in three years and the day the gulls did not come because someone started covering the fish scraps more quickly.

The tideglass approved of this—in its way. Its hum warmed when she wrote down a hinge. It quieted when she tried to wring from it a complete picture of a winter two years away. She set it on the sill morning and night and felt slightly foolish and slightly comforted, the way one feels pressing a palm to a tree just because one’s father had done so.

Elias teased her by bringing not shells but hinges: old brass ones from doors the carpenters had replaced, a little tin one from a sewing box he found at the market. “For offerings,” he said gravely. “For your shrine to useful pivot points.”

One afternoon he brought a hinge so corroded it barely resembled metal. “Found this near where the old gull statue used to be,” he said. “Thought we could polish it and make it squeak again.”

“Once a diver, always a finder,” she said, but took it and set it beside the glass.

Epilogue

The tideglass never explained itself. It did not tell Mara where it came from, who sent it, or whether it would one day crack like an ordinary thing and become harmless sand. It did not ask for worship, and it did not gloat. It showed futures like the sea shows the sky: as a mirror broken by a thousand ripples, as a promise of blue that shifts into gray.

Heron Quay learned how to read its water and its hinges a little better. The barrier, when it came, not as a triumph but as one piece of a stubborn, local atlas, had gaps where reeds rooted and a gate with a wheel that turned in the hands of people whose boots had known the bay all their lives. The promenade they built on top of the low wall had no plaques for city offices but had names carved into its rail by someone who had forgotten to ask permission: Hobb, Darya, Isla, Harrison, Priya, Elias, Mara—letters not in order; letters that would wear down under salt and fingers and time and then be cut again by someone younger.

Sometimes in the afternoon when the light was right, the glass on Mara’s sill showed the ledger and the harbor and, faintly, something like a road leading seaward and turning into a river. She never followed the road with her eyes all the way. She knew it branched. She knew it was a map of choices, not inevitabilities.

Once, after a spring storm that had shaken the windows but left the floor dry, Hobb came to the door of the Records Office and stood there a long moment, listening to the quiet. “The bell would have rung for that one,” he said.

“The glass didn’t sing at all,” Mara said.

“Maybe it knows we are listening to other things now,” Hobb said.

Mara dipped her pen and wrote the day’s numbers. When she was finished, she turned the page of the Atlas of Small Hinges and added a line that was not a measurement: The smell of the harbor at low tide has been the same since I was little: mud and rot and sweetness. It is a kind of truth that does not ask to be proved.

She set down the pen and looked out at the bay where the gulls hung like punctuation marks in a story that never quite ended. The tideglass sat beside her and caught a scrap of sunlight in one of its pale veins. It did not sing. It did not need to.

Outside, the water drew back. In a few hours it would return, slipping over the mud as if nothing had ever decided anything at all. But Mara knew better than to believe that. She had an atlas in her lap to prove otherwise, and a town full of hands that remembered the feel of hinges turning under them.
