MARKET RESEARCH BRIEF
Decision:
- Determine whether to build and launch a SaaS platform that helps commercial construction lenders manage draw schedules, site inspections, and lien waiver workflows, starting with Texas as the initial market within the next 18 months.

Target customer:
- Commercial construction lenders at regional/community banks, credit unions with commercial portfolios, and private debt funds (bridge/construction) operating in Texas.
- Primary user roles: Construction loan administrators, portfolio managers, draw specialists, and risk/credit operations.

Geography:
- Phase 1 launch: Texas (TX) only.
- Phase 2 (post-18 months, contingent on success): Adjacent states in the Sun Belt.

Category or substitute set:
- Construction loan administration software for draw management, inspection coordination, and lien waiver collection.
- Substitutes: General LOS add-ons, spreadsheets + email + SharePoint, eSignature tools, generic project management platforms, and in-house custom tools.

Time horizon:
- 18 months to an initial Texas-focused launch and first 10–30 paying institutions.

Must-answer questions:
1) How large is the reachable opportunity in Texas (SAM) and how big could our initial share realistically be within 18 months (SOM)?
2) Which competitors (direct, indirect, potential) already serve draw schedules, inspections, and lien waiver use-cases? Where is white space for a Texas-first approach?
3) What are pricing anchors and a credible entry price range? Should we price per active construction loan, per portfolio tier, or per user?
4) What adoption constraints matter most (e.g., integration with LOS/core, vendor security reviews, Texas lien law nuances)? How do DSCR/LTV/LTC/debt yield and risk policy shape the reachable segment?
5) What evidence (market structure, behavior signals, direct customer notes) supports demand and urgency? What would invalidate a go decision?

Evidence bar:
- Market-entry decision requires High confidence on demand signals and reachability.
- Minimum: Triangulation across three evidence families (market structure data, behavior data, direct customer evidence) with explicit confidence grading.

Starting assumptions for sizing (adjustable during analysis):
- U.S. baseline: ~5,000 institutions actively making commercial construction loans (banks, credit unions with commercial units, private lenders).
- Average active commercial construction loans per participating institution: ~50 nationally; Texas institutions skew higher (~100 on average) due to construction volume.
- Estimated active commercial construction loans: U.S. ~250,000; Texas ~30,000–35,000.
- Plausible SaaS pricing anchors:
  - Per active loan: $15–$30 per month
  - Portfolio-tiered annual plans: $10,000–$40,000 per institution depending on active loan count
- Operational baseline: Typical draw frequency ~1–2 per loan per month; inspections required for each disbursement; lien waivers collected from GCs and subs.

Decision criteria:
- Go if: Clear white space in Texas for mid-market lenders; at least medium-to-high confidence that 10–30 institutions can be acquired within 18 months at $8k–$20k ARR each; integration requirements manageable without bespoke per-client builds.
- No-go if: Incumbents dominate with low switching appetite, integration and legal hurdles exceed 9–12 month sales cycle for most targets, or willingness-to-pay falls below $10/loan/month or $8k ARR for typical portfolios.

Notes on domain considerations to include:
- DSCR, LTV, LTC, and debt yield influence draw cadence and oversight requirements.
- Texas lien law and waiver practices affect workflow complexity.
- Inspection SLA expectations and vendor risk reviews affect adoption and procurement.

Contacts and context:
- Early calls with TX-based community banks, a private lender, and a regional bank (see customer_notes.txt) indicate process pain around spreadsheets, audit trails, and lien waiver tracking.
- Signals from job postings and forum mentions (signals.jsonl) suggest active hiring and process modernization around construction loan administration.
