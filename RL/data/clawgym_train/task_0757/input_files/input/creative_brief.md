Genre: Magical Realism Mystery

Setting:
- Place: Greyhaven, a fog-bound fishing town on the Oregon coast. Weathered boardwalks, a tidal estuary, and the decommissioned Bellwether Light stand as landmarks. The town’s community radio station, WGHN, broadcasts late-night show “Night Tides” from a second-floor studio overlooking the harbor.
- Texture: Salt-wet timber, gull-shadowed mornings, and an old lighthouse lens that still throws prismatic ghosts across the water when polished. Tide clocks hang in shop windows, but at dusk some quietly insist on the wrong hour.
- Everyday life: Net-mending at the pier, a bookshop that doubles as a chartroom, and a diner that closes whenever the foghorn blows twice in quick succession.

Tone, POV, and Tense:
- Tone: Melancholic but hopeful—aching with old griefs, yet tender toward small repairs and second chances.
- POV: Third-person limited, centered primarily on Mara Ellison.
- Tense: Past.

Length and Structure:
- Story length: 2,400–3,000 words.
- Chapters: At least 4 chapters with headings exactly like “## Chapter 1”, “## Chapter 2”, etc.

Magical Realism Ground Rules:
- Memory-objects: Certain salt-touched items briefly show what’s missing rather than what’s there—sea glass flashes silhouettes; tide-stained letters stay cool as river stones.
- Time-slippage: Radios at WGHN sometimes broadcast voices a beat out of time at 2:17 a.m.; the Bellwether lens, when polished with brine, refracts the present to reveal the shadow of what has been taken.
- The town does not question the uncanny; it weaves around it, using it in small, practical ways. Magic never solves the mystery outright; it tilts ordinary evidence into focus.

Central Mystery:
- Thirty years ago, on the night of September 17, 1994, the lighthouse light went dark for seven minutes. The ferry Ardor grazed a shoal. Amid confusion, a teenager—Rowan Hart—vanished. No body was found. Rumors of sabotage and guilt calcified between families.
- Present day: Mara Ellison returns to Greyhaven to settle her grandmother’s effects. Among the boxes is a sealed, salt-stiff envelope addressed to “Eloise Hart.” Inside: a fragmentary letter and spidery marks that resemble tide tables. Meanwhile, at dusk, several town tide clocks show the wrong hour in the same direction.

Required Characters:
- Mara Ellison: A 34-year-old archivist between jobs, returning to Greyhaven with a box of keys and a cautious heart.
- Jonah Pike: A fisherman and volunteer caretaker of Bellwether Light. Practical and private, he is tender with broken things and reticent with himself.
- Eloise Hart: Late-night radio host of “Night Tides.” Keeper of the town’s living memory; older, sharp-witted, and careful about her own past. She is related to Rowan Hart.

Themes (guide, not exhaustive):
- Grief and repair: How small acts of witness restore frayed trust.
- Belonging: The slow work of claiming and being claimed by a place.
- Truth and its costs: The difference between a lie and a borrowed name.
- Memory as tide: What returns; what recedes; what is carried in salt.

Voice and Style Constraints:
- Melancholic but hopeful tone throughout.
- Third-person limited POV (center Mara), past tense.
- Magical realism must be woven into a grounded investigation: clues, interviews, interpretations, and consequences.
- Avoid explicit gore. The sea can be dangerous without being graphic.

Key Beats to Cover (flexible ordering that respects causality):
1) Arrival: Mara reaches Greyhaven with her grandmother’s boxes and opens the letter for Eloise Hart; it smells faintly of brine and old paper. At dusk, a tide clock insists on the wrong hour.
2) Radio: Mara takes the letter to Eloise’s “Night Tides” studio; a caller’s voice arrives a beat early, or late, at 2:17 a.m., naming “Bellwether” and “seven minutes.”
3) Lighthouse: Jonah Pike, caretaker of Bellwether Light, helps Mara into the lantern room. When they polish the lens with salt, refracted light suggests what is missing: a lamp lit too late, a lantern seen one beat too late, a figure stepping back rather than forward.
4) Community: Whispered rumors resurface—sabotage, a teenage dare, an off-air call Eloise once cut short. Someone leaves tide-stained paper on Mara’s stoop.
5) Storm and Revelation: During a squall the radio repeats a fragment backwards; sea glass flashes silhouettes; footprints fill with rain at the lighthouse stairs; the “borrowed name” motif rises to the surface.
6) Confrontation and Twist: The truth reframes the central mystery: Jonah Pike is Rowan Hart, living under a borrowed name since the seven-minute darkness. He turned the light off to prevent a greater catastrophe after a misread beacon; he stayed to keep the town safe, trading a name for the work of repair. Eloise suspected and kept his secret, believing confession would harm more than heal.
7) Resolution: Mara acts as witness, not judge. The town’s clocks reconcile slowly. The ending is emotionally resonant and hopeful: not all debts can be paid, but they can be acknowledged; repair continues.
