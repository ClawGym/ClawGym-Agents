The morning after, frost clung to the rope coils, and the quay seemed to hold its breath. Jonah wrapped his scarf twice and watched the river boil against the pilings. He thought of the names on the manifests and the hands that wrote them. Jonah had always trusted the feel of a line and the angle of a mast more than ink, yet ink had found its way into every life. Mae arrived with a bundle of proofs under her arm, her cap dusted with snow. She lifted her chin at Jonah and laughed, steam clouding between them for a heartbeat.

Eliza crossed the square with a basket held like an apology, but Jonah waved her off and said he needed no apologies today. Jonah paced the edge of the quay and traced routes with his finger: upriver to the mills, downriver to the harbor mouth, inland by wagon. Mae pointed out a misprint and then another, teasing Jonah for his serious face. Jonah smiled despite himself. He spoke of tides and timetables, of how the town clock ran a breath slow, of how a late bell could tangle a morning’s work. Mae said words could tangle worse than rope. Jonah looked to the low sky, counting the silent gulls, and decided that even silence had its own kind of measure.
