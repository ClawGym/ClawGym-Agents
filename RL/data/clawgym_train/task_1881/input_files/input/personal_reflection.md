Personal Reflection — Draft

I became a legislator after years of working in community settings where the human stories outweighed the statistics. My training in psychology taught me to look for patterns that signal change: moments when stigma gives way to curiosity, when anxiety is named and therefore becomes manageable.

Growing up, my family relied on a community clinic. A school counselor helped me learn to challenge all-or-nothing thinking. That experience shapes how I approach policy—investing in early supports, validating lived experience, and insisting on outcomes.

Ahead of the town hall, I hoped to hear whether access was expanding or contracting for families, veterans, and students. I also wanted to understand whether our policies around telehealth are catching up with clinical realities.

This is a working note. It needs to incorporate what we learned, translate those insights into tangible steps, and connect them to evidence-based practices like cognitive-behavioral strategies and behavioral activation.
