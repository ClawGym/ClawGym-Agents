Priority themes and chapters to emphasize in the conversation:

1) Good Friction vs. Bad Friction (Ch. 1–2)
- Core idea: Not all friction is waste. “Good friction” creates productive resistance that prevents errors, prompts reflection, and aligns actions with values. “Bad friction” is clutter that impedes progress without adding safety, clarity, or meaning.
- Practical test: ask “Does this step change outcomes or only slow throughput?”
- Conversation prompt: examples where removing friction backfired (e.g., one-click mistakes, misfires in automation), and when adding small steps improved quality or ethics.

2) Micro-Pauses and Checklists as Cognitive Seatbelts (Ch. 3–4)
- Micro-pauses: 3–7 second intentional interruptions before irreversible actions (“Commit” vs “Confirm”).
- Checklists: short, situationally-aware lists (5–9 items) that prevent “fast brain” errors; adapted from aviation/medicine.
- Conversation prompt: how to design micro-pauses that feel respectful, not patronizing; how to keep checklists living and contextual.

3) Defaults, Choice Architecture, and the “Attention Tax” (Ch. 5)
- Defaults reflect values; “silent defaults” can entrench risky or unethical outcomes.
- Attention tax: when interfaces offload cognitive cost onto users; the book argues designers should “pay” the tax through thoughtful defaults and well-placed speed bumps.
- Conversation prompt: ethics of nudge vs sludge; how to audit defaults.

4) Case Studies and Story Anchors (Ch. 6–7)
- Hospital medication “five rights” with a 6-second read-back added; error rate dropped.
- “Two-clicks-to-doom” fintech bug: removing a confirmation screen increased accidental transfers by 27% until friction was restored.
- Aviation sterile cockpit rule and the descent checklist redesign.
- Conversation prompt: what made these interventions work technically and culturally?

5) Rituals, Meaning, and Designed Deliberate Moments (Ch. 8)
- Rituals as human-centered friction that build identity, trust, and belonging.
- “Start-of-shift huddles” and “commit moments” (e.g., reading user intent aloud in critical ops) as tools for alignment.
- Conversation prompt: when rituals become empty theater; signs you need to prune.

6) Implementation Playbook and Metrics (Ch. 9–10)
- Friction-mapping audit: catalog high-stakes decisions, observe error patterns, target micro-pauses/checklists where consequence is high and velocity is risky.
- A/B Pause Tests: insert brief confirmations for a cohort, measure error rates, abandonment, and satisfaction.
- Metrics beyond speed: measure “Right-First-Time,” near-misses, sentiment, and trust.
- Conversation prompt: avoiding over-friction; sunset clauses for every added step.

Clarifying questions the host might ask during the show:
- How to tell if friction is respectful safety or condescending gatekeeping?
- What’s a 60-minute “friction audit” any team can run this week?
- Where have organizations successfully removed bad friction while adding good friction?
- What’s a simple rubric for deciding when to ship a confirmation dialog vs undo?

Remember to include:
- At least one respectful challenge about “speed vs. safety trade-offs in competitive markets.”
- One “I never thought of it that way” moment tied to defaults/ethics.
- “well, actually” clarification from the guest about checklists or micro-pauses.
