# Northloop Analytics — Brand Voice Guide
Last updated: 2026-04-15

About
Northloop Analytics builds tools for data engineers and analytics leaders who care about reliability. Our users are technical and time-constrained. They prefer clear writing over hype, specifics over slogans.

Voice pillars (use these when writing)
1) Plainspoken and specific
- We are: direct, concrete, and concise. We prefer “what changed and why” over sweeping statements.
- We are not: grandiose, vague, or promotional.
- Sounds like: “Query Snapshots capture SQL text, parameters, and result metadata so teammates can reproduce an issue.”
- Does NOT sound like: “This feature stands as a testament to our enduring commitment to innovation.”

2) Evidence-led and honest about tradeoffs
- We are: transparent about limits, expected impact, and edge cases.
- We are not: hand-wavy, overly certain, or evasive.
- Sounds like: “Beta teams saw build times drop from 18m to 14m on average; two teams saw no change due to warehouse limits.”
- Does NOT sound like: “Industry experts believe this will transform the analytics landscape.”

3) Friendly, not chummy
- We are: approachable and respectful. Light humor is fine. No cheerleading.
- We are not: chatty assistants or salesy hype machines.
- Sounds like: “If you hit a flaky test, start with the freshness check—we flag the table that’s most likely the culprit.”
- Does NOT sound like: “Great question! Of course we can help with anything and everything!”

4) Technical clarity over flair
- We are: precise about how things work. Simple sentences beat fancy phrasing.
- We are not: obsessed with clever turns of phrase or em dashes.
- Sounds like: “We compare row counts over a 7-day window and alert on a 3σ deviation.”
- Does NOT sound like: “We showcase a vibrant interplay of signals across your evolving data tapestry.”

5) Consistent rhythm
- We are: varied in sentence length. We avoid list-of-three clichés and filler transitions.
- We are not: monotone or bullet-point robots.
- Sounds like: “Start small. Monitor the top five models. Expand once the alerts are useful.”
- Does NOT sound like: “It’s fast, intuitive, and powerful.”

Style rules
- Headings: sentence case
- Oxford comma: yes
- Contractions: use them when natural
- Numbers: numerals for 10+, words for one–nine unless a unit is attached (e.g., 5 ms)
- Quotes: straight quotes only ("like this")
- Dashes: prefer commas or periods over em dashes
- Emphasis: no bold or emoji in body copy
- Links: descriptive text, not “click here”
- Avoid these words unless in a quote or code: additionally, landscape, pivotal, underscore, testament, showcase, vibrant, boasts, serves as, stands as, enduring, commitment to, broader, fostering, enhance
- Endings: do not finish with a generic upbeat conclusion. Close with a concrete next step or leave it factual.

Audience notes
- Primary: data engineers, analytics engineers, platform teams
- Secondary: data leaders who review and approve changes
- Tone shifts: documentation is neutral and precise; blog is slightly more conversational; newsletters are brief, concrete, and friendly

Examples to emulate
- “We ship small changes weekly and call out risk. If something breaks, we say so and how to fix it.”
- “If a metric flips due to an upstream rename, we link the commit and the failing test.”
