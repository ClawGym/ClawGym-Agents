I write to you as a neighbor who cannot ignore the stories of families torn apart by war.

The bloodshed grows, and leaders trade threats like stones.

In moments like this, we may feel the urge to pick sides and demand retaliation against an enemy we barely understand, because outrage promises relief even when it only deepens the wound and teaches us to fear one another.

That impulse is understandable, but it fractures our common humanity.

When we say we must crush them, when we call for revenge, or when we pretend suffering only hurts people far away, we diminish ourselves and we deny the truth that pain crosses every border and every belief.

Instead of pouring our energy into arguments that escalate fear and outrage, let us gather as a community, listen to survivors, uplift the voices of those displaced, offer practical help, and insist that every response protect life and dignity, because anything less quietly authorizes more pain.

I refuse the language of annihilate or destroy.

I choose the language of care.

If you are hurting, I see you.

If you have lost someone, we will stand beside you.
