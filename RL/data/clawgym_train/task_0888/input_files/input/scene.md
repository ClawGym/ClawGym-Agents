NARRATION: Madison Avenue, late afternoon. The copy desk hums like a beehive.
Lila: The proofs look sharp, but we need to A/B test the headline.
Jack: A/B test? On which lab bench, kid? We have a press run in the morning.
Lila: Sorry—habit. I mean, maybe try two headlines at the department store counter.
Jack: Now you're talking. One benefit, one heartbeat.
NARRATION: He leans closer, the scent of turpentine and aftershave.
Lila: "Starlight Lipstick: Kiss-proof color, breakfast-bright"—does it sing?
Jack: It hums. Maybe nudge "breakfast-bright" to "dawn-bright"? Dawn has a softer neck.
Lila: The client wants bite-sized content for socials, though.
Jack: For what? The ladies at the soda fountain don't read "socials." They read faces.
Lila: You're right. Maybe we pivot after we see the metrics from the launch.
Jack: We see sales, not metrics. The counter girls will tell us in an hour.
NARRATION: A neon flicker drizzles across the layout.
Lila: I'm worried the copy feels extra long; our brand story needs more synergy.
Jack: Save "synergy" for the fellas inventing space rockets.
Lila: Okay. "Kiss-proof color that keeps its promise from the first coffee to the last dance."
Jack: That's the ticket. Keep it crisp.
Lila: And maybe we can iterate on the tagline next week after we gather feedback.
Jack: Next week we'll be dancing. Tonight we need one line that winks.
NARRATION: He taps a corner of the mock-up, his finger almost grazing hers.
Lila: Jack, if we launch big, we could disrupt the category.
Jack: We don't disrupt. We simply look better in the mirror.
Lila: Sorry. I just want to make it clear, concise, and sticky.
Jack: Then write it like a note you'd slip into a pocket.
Lila: "Starlight: A little color that keeps a promise." Too old-fashioned?
Jack: Just right.
