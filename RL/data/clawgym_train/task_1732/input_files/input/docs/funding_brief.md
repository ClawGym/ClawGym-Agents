# Open Data Microgrants for Animal Health Surveillance (Pilot)

## Context
We keep encountering a recurring and frustrating but solvable problem in animal disease work: the field and lab teams are collecting important surveillance data in ways that are pretty ad hoc; as a result, when it's time to analyze or share, the information is inconsistent, poorly labeled, or hard to combine. This slows everybody down, creates waste, and in a small organization like ours, it also makes it harder to confidently support smart decisions.

## Rationale
Rather than attempting to build a giant system, we want to test whether very small, fast, and practical grants can quickly improve basic data hygiene in place (think: short SOPs; better spreadsheet headers; basic codebooks; simple training). This is not glamorous, but we believe it unlocks value.

## Pilot scope (June–August 2026)
- 6–8 microgrants, each up to $7,500.
- Target: field teams and small labs working on zoonotic surveillance in low-resource settings.
- Focus: concrete artifacts that make datasets more usable and sharable without heavy software.

## Selection criteria (draft)
- Relevance to animal disease surveillance and near-term data usability.
- Evidence that the team can deliver within 8–10 weeks.
- A specific artifact to be produced (e.g., a metadata template, a data dictionary, or a lightweight SOP).
- Sensible, minimal budget.

## Reporting and success measures
We want to keep reporting simple; we are thinking of two or three metrics that reflect use and adoption, along with a short narrative summary and a link to any artifacts that can be openly shared, respecting ethics and privacy.

## Risks and mitigations
The biggest risk is trying to do too much; another is producing documents that nobody uses. We'll counter by keeping scope small, co-designing with end users, and asking grantees to show evidence of adoption (e.g., training sign-in sheets, template usage screenshots).
