By the third night, snow had traced every sill and signboard, and Eliza’s footsteps stitched a path between shop and alley. Candles burned low in the printer’s room, and Mae rubbed warmth into her fingers before setting the form again. Eliza read each line aloud, listening for the false notes that fear might slip into the type. The press groaned and answered. Outside, a cart wheel stuck in rutted ice and then lurched free.

Magistrate Perkins passed beneath the window with two constables at his heel, and Eliza’s voice fell to a whisper. She knew the cadence of his speech almost as well as her own name; she had watched him on market days tallying fines as if each coin smoothed a wrinkle from his brow. Eliza did not believe that money could ever iron the world flat. She believed in hands, in hinges, in the way doors admitted or refused. Jonah appeared with his coat powdered white, and he shook the snow from his shoulders like a dog. He grinned at Mae, at Eliza, and then sobered when he saw the stack of damp sheets. Eliza pressed another page and counted to five while the ink took. She thought of morning routes and who would carry which roll to which corner. She thought of the gaps in the week—the way a single day could yawn like a bridge out—and chose where to lay the next plank.
